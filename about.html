Our founders' story.

Kelvi began as a shared discomfort. In their classrooms, meetings, and long conversations over plates of rice, rasam, and thoran, Gayatri Ramesh and Padmaja Ganapathy kept returning to the same question: why does so much math technology care more about answers than about how students think?

Most tools rushed learners toward correctness, wrapped in dashboards and scores. Very few lingered with a student's question long enough to see the reasoning underneath. Over time, between lesson plans, research papers, and shared meals, Gayatri and Padmaja decided they wanted something different: a space where the central object of care was not the answer, but the question.

Gayatri and Padmaja first met at Phillips Exeter Academy, when Gayatri was teaching Padmaja's son, Gautham, in her Fourier Analysis class. What began as conversations about his mathematical thinking quickly expanded into longer discussions about pedagogy, equity, and what it feels like to learn with real uncertainty on the table. Over time, their families became close, and the questions that would eventually become Kelvi started to show up in car rides, school concerts, and dinners as often as in classrooms and meetings.

Gayatri: questions in the classroom.

Gayatri's path into Kelvi runs through years of teaching and listening to students think aloud about mathematics. With a PhD in Applied Mathematics from the University of Central Florida and roles as faculty at Phillips Exeter Academy, Chair of Mathematics at The Branson School, and Assistant Dean of Faculty at Exeter, she has lived inside many versions of "rigorous" math education.

What stayed with her was not the number of problems completed, but the kinds of questions students learned to ask. In seminars, in office hours, and in quiet moments after class, she watched understanding deepen when students were pushed to name patterns, connect ideas, and generate their own variations on a problem. Those same kinds of questions often spilled over into evenings with Padmaja, the conversation flowing as easily as the ladle of rasam over a mound of rice.

Padmaja: data, systems, and care.

Padmaja's path into Kelvi comes from a different angle: science, analytics, and the work of building systems that matter in the real world. She holds a PhD in Cancer Biology and an MBA from Rutgers, and she serves as CEO of Sathgen Therapeutics. Her work has always sat at the intersection of complex data, high stakes, and human decisions.

In education, she saw a familiar tension. There was no shortage of data about students, but very little of it honored how learning actually feels from the inside. Together with Gayatri, often at a kitchen table with thoran on the side and a half-scribbled diagram between them, she began to ask: what would it look like to design an educational tool that tracks thinking without turning learners into data points to be optimized?

They chose the name Kelvi — Tamil for "question" — to keep themselves honest. If the product ever drifts toward quick answers, correctness scores, or age-based sorting, it has stopped being what they set out to build.

Building Kelvi together.

Kelvi is where those lines of questioning meet. From Gayatri comes the insistence that math conversations should be Socratic, slow enough to notice reasoning, and generous with error. From Padmaja comes the discipline to turn that pedagogy into a product that can run in a simple, transparent technical stack and still respond intelligently in real time.

The same way a shared meal of rice, rasam, and thoran anchors them back to something simple and essential, the name anchors the product back to its purpose.

What they chose to build.

Together, Gayatri and Padmaja designed Kelvi as an edtech platform that develops mathematical questioning and reasoning rather than delivering solutions. Instead of confirming correctness, the AI asks carefully chosen follow-ups that help learners examine their work, notice patterns, and build confidence in validating their own reasoning.

Kelvi School, Kelvi Family, and Kelvi Solo are three expressions of the same idea: give teachers, guardians, and independent learners a space where mathematical voice can grow, one question at a time. The visual language is warm and minimal on purpose, with no badges or streaks, because the work it is trying to honor is internal.

Kelvi in our own homes.

Kelvi is not only something Gayatri and Padmaja build; it is something they use. Gayatri uses Kelvi with her daughters, Arya and Luna, to sit with problems a little longer, name patterns out loud, and let uncertainty be part of doing mathematics together. Those evenings at the table are one of the ways the product is tested: if it cannot support real conversations with real children they love, it is not finished yet.

Why this story matters.

For Gayatri and Padmaja, Kelvi is not a side project or a generic AI wrapper. It is the product of years spent with students, data, and decisions — and of many small, ordinary moments together, talking about learning over rice, rasam, and thoran. Underneath it is a stubborn belief: the hardest part of learning math is not finding answers, but being allowed to ask and refine better questions.

Kelvi is their answer to that belief — a platform built so that, in every interaction, the question is the answer.
