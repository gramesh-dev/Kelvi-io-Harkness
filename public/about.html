Our founders' story.

Gayatri Ramesh and Padmaja Ganapathy met serendipitously on the campus of Phillips Exeter Academy in the small town of Exeter, New Hampshire.

Padmaja and her family had moved to Exeter so her son Gautham could attend Phillips Exeter Academy (PEA), one of the best high schools in the country. Gayatri and her family — including her two young daughters, Arya and Luna — had also just arrived. After completing a PhD in Applied Mathematics at the University of Central Florida, Gayatri had tried the corporate world of financial mathematics and quickly realized it wasn’t hers. Education was. She joined Exeter’s math department and athletics program. As a former Division I tennis player, she cherished the opportunity to follow both her passions — mathematics and tennis.

The two met because Gautham happened to land in Gayatri’s Fourier Analysis class. What started as a parent-teacher relationship quickly blossomed into a beautiful friendship. The two families share a South Indian identity, and with it comes the love of idly and sambar and other mouthwatering dishes that turned every visit into a long one. After Gautham’s graduation, the families stayed close.

Padmaja and Gayatri were always ideating — whether it was education, science, business, or parenting. Recently, their conversations turned to the ever-changing landscape of AI. Both are early adopters. They share a love of learning and a belief that change is the only constant. Padmaja uses AI in her work as a cancer biologist and as the founder of Artsee. Gayatri uses AI as a thinking partner in her classroom and in her day-to-day life to stay on top of the thousand things a teacher juggles. Somewhere between those conversations, a sharper question emerged: why does so much math technology care more about answers than about how students think?

The shared discomfort.

Most math tools rush learners toward correctness, wrap results in dashboards and scores, and move on. Very few linger with a student’s question long enough to see the reasoning underneath.

This bothered them for different reasons.

Gayatri had spent years teaching and listening to students think aloud about mathematics — as faculty at Exeter, as Chair of Mathematics at The Branson School, and as Assistant Dean of Faculty. What stayed with her was never the number of problems completed. It was the kinds of questions students learned to ask. Understanding deepened when students named patterns, connected ideas, and generated their own variations on a problem. She wanted a tool that honored that.

Padmaja saw the same gap from the other side. With a PhD in Cancer Biology, an MBA from Rutgers, and years leading data-intensive work — as CEO of Sathgen Therapeutics, VP at Godavari BioRefineries, and Senior Portfolio Analyst at Johnson & Johnson — she understood what it means to build systems around complex, high-stakes data. In education, there was no shortage of data about students. But very little of it honored how learning actually feels from the inside.

Together, they began to ask: what would it look like to design something that tracks thinking without turning learners into data points to be optimized?

They chose the name Kelvi — கேள்வி, Tamil for “question” — to keep themselves honest. If the product ever drifts toward quick answers, correctness scores, or age-based sorting, it has stopped being what they set out to build.

What they built.

Kelvi is an edtech platform that develops mathematical questioning and reasoning rather than delivering solutions. Instead of confirming whether an answer is right, the AI asks carefully chosen follow-ups that help learners examine their own work, notice patterns, and build confidence in validating their own thinking.

From Gayatri comes the insistence that math conversations should be Socratic — slow enough to notice reasoning, generous with error, and always more interested in the question than in the answer. From Padmaja comes the discipline to turn that pedagogy into a product that runs in a clean, transparent technical stack and responds intelligently in real time.

Three products, one conviction: Kelvi School gives teachers a window into how their students think. Kelvi Family gives parents and young children a space to wonder together. Kelvi Student gives independent learners a companion that never gives up on their question.

Kelvi in our own homes.

Kelvi is not only something Gayatri and Padmaja build. It is something they use. Gayatri works with Arya and Luna to sit with problems a little longer, name patterns out loud, and let uncertainty be part of doing mathematics together. Those evenings are one of the ways the product is tested: if it cannot support real conversations with real children they love, it is not finished yet.

Why this matters.

Most children decide whether they are “a math person” by age eight or nine — and that decision is set by experience, not ability. Kelvi intervenes before that identity fixes. Not with gamification, not with scores, not with age gates. With questions.

The tagline is the whole philosophy: the question is the answer.
